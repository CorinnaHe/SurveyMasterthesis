Your Task

In this study, you will take the role of a bank employee assessing credit applications.

In each case, you will see information about an applicant, such as income, debt, and employment details.

Your task is to classify each applicant into one of the following categories:

  • Poor creditworthiness
  • Standard creditworthiness
  • Good creditworthiness

Please make each decision carefully based on the information shown.

How Each Case Works

Each case consists of two stages:

Stage 1:
You will first make a decision based only on the applicant information, without any AI support.

Stage 2:
After that, you will see an AI-based assessment of the same case. You can then keep or change your decision.

Important:
You always make the final decision.
The AI provides support, but it does not decide for you.

Applicant Information Overview

In each case, you will be shown information about an applicant. Below is an overview of the types of information you will see and what they represent.

Sociodemographic information

  • Age: Age of the individual in years
  • Occupation: Occupational category of the individual

Financial situation

  • Annual income: Annual gross income of the individual
  • Outstanding debt: Total amount of outstanding financial liabilities
  • Monthly balance: Average monthly account balance

Credit-related information

  • Number of credit cards: Number of credit cards currently held
  • Interest rate: The percentage charged on the applicant’s existing credit, which determines how much extra they have to pay on borrowed money over time. A higher interest rate means borrowing is more expensive for the applicant.

About the AI System

The AI system is trained on past credit decisions and looks for patterns in financial information.

For each applicant, it considers the same factors that will be shown to you, such as annual income, outstanding debt, and interest rates.

Some factors tend to influence the AI’s assessment more strongly than others. The visualization below shows the most influential factors on average across many cases.

Importantly, the AI does not always make correct predictions.

Your task is therefore not to follow the AI automatically, but to use its assessment as decision support when making your final judgment.

How the AI Communicates Its Assessment

{% if condition == "C1" %}

To assist your decision, an AI model has been trained to assess applicants based on patterns learned from similar past cases.

For each case, the model provides a single suggested outcome.

Along with this suggestion, the model reports how often predictions of this type were correct in similar past cases, using a frequency-based description (for example, “82 out of 100 cases”).

This information reflects the model’s uncertainty based on past data, not a guarantee for the current case.

The model can make mistakes. You should therefore rely on your own judgment and make the final decision.

{% elif condition == "C2" %}

To assist your decision, an AI model has been trained to assess applicants based on patterns learned from similar past cases.

For each case, the model provides a single suggested outcome.

In addition, the model communicates its uncertainty using a qualitative confidence category (low, medium, or high confidence).

These confidence categories summarize how certain the model is about its prediction based on similar past cases, but they do not guarantee correctness for the current decision.

The model can be wrong. You should use your own judgment and make the final decision, even if you disagree with the AI’s suggestion.

{% elif condition == "C3" %}

To assist your decision, an AI model has been trained to assess applicants based on patterns learned from similar past cases.

For each case, the model provides a set of multiple outcomes that it considers plausible, rather than recommending a single outcome.

The model has been evaluated on similar past cases. In about 95 out of 100 cases, the true outcome was included in the set shown by the model.

When the AI presents more possible outcomes, this indicates that the case is more uncertain for the model. When it presents fewer outcomes, the model is more confident based on similar past cases.

Your task is to make the final decision using your own judgment, considering the set as decision support rather than a single recommendation.

Note: The order of outcomes reflects how strongly the model associates them with the case, but all listed outcomes should be considered plausible.
